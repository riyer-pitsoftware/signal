# Priya V. — Director of Customer Engineering, enterprise SaaS

## Background

Director of Customer Engineering at Northrise Software, an enterprise data-quality SaaS, ~700 people. I have a team of 11 — six senior CEs, three solutions architects, two principals. I report to the SVP of Customer Success who reports to the CRO. I've been here about four years; made director eighteen months ago.

The job: my team handles the technical-implementation side of our top 40 enterprise customers. We don't sell. The AEs sell. We do everything between contract signature and the customer being self-sufficient on the platform — which for our customers usually means a six-to-nine month implementation cycle. We ladder up through the customer's data org, and our success is measured roughly in time-to-first-value (typically 90 days for the lighthouse use case) and renewal rate.

I want to be careful about what I claim because the job is fundamentally about influence and the influence is hard to evidence cleanly.

What I think I do well: I've structured the implementation playbook in a way that the org actually follows. When I joined as a CE in 2021, the team had four "playbooks" that contradicted each other and that nobody used because every implementation was treated as bespoke. I rewrote them down to one in 2022 — not by writing it from scratch but by sitting with our three top CEs and getting them to articulate what they actually did, which they hadn't ever written down. The new playbook is six pages, plus an appendix of customer archetypes. Our time-to-first-value across the top 40 went from a 5-month median to a 3-month median in the year after deployment. Renewal rate held at 94%, which is normal for our segment.

But here's what I'm honest about: I cannot prove the playbook caused the time-to-value improvement. Three things changed in 2022. The product matured (a lot of bespoke implementation work became unnecessary). We changed our pricing model from per-seat to per-data-volume, which let smaller use cases land first instead of waiting for full deployment. And the playbook landed. Any of those three could account for most of the improvement. The CRO gives me credit for the playbook because that's the credit-assignment that makes sense to him; I have always told him it's at most a third of the story.

What I do daily: I'm in 1:1s with our three highest-risk accounts roughly weekly, on the customer's side directly with their VP of data. I'm not the assigned CE for those accounts; the assigned CE leads the work. I'm the relationship manager for the strategic layer — the conversations the customer has when something is going wrong and they don't yet want to escalate to our CRO. About 60% of those conversations end without anything formal happening. The customer just needed to hear that someone above the CE was aware and thinking about their problem. The other 40% become an escalation that I help shape on our side before the customer formally raises it.

The work that doesn't show up anywhere: I think I prevent about three to five renewal losses per year by virtue of those conversations. I cannot prove this number. I have a list of the renewals I think I made-the-difference on, and I'd argue for them, but the counterfactual isn't real. The CRO knows, in some less-precise way, that this work is happening. The CFO does not.

Behavior I'm working on: I am too willing to take on the strategic-layer relationship myself rather than developing my senior CEs to do it. Two of my senior CEs have flagged that they want more exposure to those conversations. I have not yet figured out how to delegate the work without cutting the customer off from what they came for, which is the highest-altitude voice they can get without going to the CRO. This is a real coaching gap and I have it on my own development plan with my SVP.

If you ask me what I'm best at: I am good at being the second-most-senior person in a customer's data org's mental model. The most-senior is our CRO, who they meet twice a year. I'm the person they think about when they're trying to decide whether to escalate. That's a specific position and I think I hold it well. I don't think it's the same job as a typical director of customer engineering.
