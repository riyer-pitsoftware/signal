# Priya S. — Principal DevRel / developer advocate, observability tooling

## Background

I'm a principal at a Series C observability company — Tracelane — about 350 people total. I'm in DevRel but with a weird carve-out: half my time is technical content, half is what we internally call "design partner success," which means I'm the one who sits with our biggest accounts when they're integrating us and I figure out what they actually need to make us indispensable. I report into the CEO directly which is unusual for a DevRel role and is honestly a sign of how confused our org structure is, but it works.

The honest thing about my career is I don't ship a lot of code. I never did, really. I came up as a backend engineer at Stripe in like 2017-2019 and I was a fine engineer, not great. What I figured out about myself was that I read other people's code very fast and I write extremely well, and those two things together make a job that companies don't really know how to hire for. I'm a translation layer. I take what our engineering team is building and I figure out which six developers at our top accounts need to know about it, and I make sure they know in the form they need it. Sometimes that's a blog post. Sometimes it's a Slack DM. Sometimes it's me sitting in a 3-hour design review with a customer's principal engineer, half listening and half writing the doc that will let her make the case internally.

I think I've materially shifted three product decisions at Tracelane. I'm going to name them but I want to be careful because none of these are mine in the way an engineer would say they own a thing. (1) The decision to build OTLP-native instead of pivoting to a proprietary protocol in 2023 — I had a six-month back-channel conversation with three principal engineers at our biggest accounts and I brought their position to our CTO before the engineering org had even surfaced the question. (2) The deprecation cadence for our v1 API — I argued for a 24-month sunset against a 12-month proposal and I won that on the basis of one specific customer story I'd documented six months prior, before anyone knew it would matter. (3) The way our pricing changed in late 2024 to per-host instead of per-event for the bottom tier. That one I'm less sure of because I think the CFO was already heading there, but I provided the customer evidence that made it move from Q4 to Q2.

The hard part of my job is that I have nothing to point to. My git history is mostly typos and YAML. I don't have shipped products. The way you know I'm doing my job is that other people's products go better. That's a hard thing to put on a resume.

I think I'm at a career inflection because at some point you have to either get your own P&L or you become a perpetual staff/principal. I've been principal for 18 months. The next move is harder than the previous moves were and I'm not sure what direction it goes.

The other thing I want to say is — I think most of my "influence" I learn about retroactively. I'll be in a meeting six months later and someone will say "we did this because Priya's customer call in March," and half the time I don't remember the call. I'm not sure if that means I'm running on instinct or if it means I'm taking credit for things that would have happened anyway.
