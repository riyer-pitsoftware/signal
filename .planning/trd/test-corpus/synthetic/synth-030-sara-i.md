# Sara I. — Staff research engineer, climate modeling

## Background

Staff research engineer at Pendulum Climate, a small climate-modeling company doing high-resolution regional ocean models for coastal-resilience planning. ~45 people, ~12 in technology. I have been here four years. Made staff in 2024. I'm one of four engineers who work directly with our climate scientists; the rest of the team builds product and infrastructure.

I want to give you the contradictory-praise picture because I've been thinking about it a lot.

Inside our company I'm well-regarded. The CEO has called me "the engineer who keeps the science honest." The chief scientist publishes papers I'm an author on. I got the highest comp-adjustment among non-management technical staff last year. We had a 3-month period last summer where I was the de-facto lead on our biggest deliverable — a hindcast/projection coupling for a coastal-engineering firm — and the work landed and the customer renewed for two more years.

Outside our company, in the climate-modeling community I came up in, I have a complicated reputation. I left academic climate science in 2019 to work at Pendulum. Several of my former colleagues think the work I do at Pendulum is, basically, climate science with shortcuts. I have heard this said in poster sessions when I have been five feet away. The specific critique is that we use simplified parameterizations for sub-grid-scale processes that the academic community has — correctly — argued are not always defensible. I know the argument is not always defensible. I make it anyway because the alternative is a model that takes 14 hours to run a single forecast and our customer needs to ingest forecasts hourly during hurricane season. The simplification is the product.

The honest version of who I am: I am an academically-trained climate scientist who became an engineer-with-domain-knowledge and who builds modeling systems that are *useful* to a specific customer at the cost of being *imperfect* by the standards of the field I came from.

Inside Pendulum, the trade-off is loved. The simplifications are the reason we have a product. The chief scientist is herself an academic-turned-applied-modeler who agrees with my approach and protects me from any internal pressure to be more academically rigorous than the customer needs.

In the academic community, the trade-off is suspected. I am asked at conferences why we don't run a higher-resolution baseline. I have an answer (we did once, on internal funding, and it confirmed the parameterization was within 8% of the high-res baseline at the spatial scale our customer cares about). But the answer doesn't always satisfy. There's a scientific-community-cultural reading that what I do is concession-to-commerce, and I am visibly on the wrong side of that cultural line.

What I'm proudest of: the parameterization-validation work I led in Q3 2023, where we ran a controlled study of our simplified model against three high-resolution baselines (one academic, one operational, one our own). We published the results — including the cases where our simplification *did* fail — at the JTECH journal and I think it's the only paper I've put my name on that I'd defend without qualification. It's also the work I most often reference when an academic asks me how I sleep at night.

What I'm not great at: managing the political weather of the dual-reputation. I have, in the past two years, declined two adjunct teaching opportunities at the institution I came from. I did not have a good reason. The honest reason is I didn't want to spend a semester walking past former mentors who disagree with my career choice. That is not a defensible reason; it's a real one.

The CEO tells me I'm undervaluing the bridge I represent between the academic community and the applied-modeling industry. He thinks I should be giving more talks, taking more visiting positions, putting myself in the rooms where the contradictory-praise pattern would be visible and could be talked about. He's probably right. I have not yet acted on it.

What I think I'm best at: building the modeling system that serves the actual decision (coastal infrastructure investment, evacuation planning) rather than the perfect-on-paper modeling system. What I am bad at: explaining this without sounding like I'm dismissing the academic critique that I think is, half the time, correct.
