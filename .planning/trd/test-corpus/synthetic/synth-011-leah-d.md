# Leah D. — VP of Engineering Programs, aerospace

## Background

VP of Engineering Programs at Halcyon Aerospace, a small-sat constellation operator. About 600 people. Three of seven of our birds are mine in the sense that I owned the program from kickoff through orbit insertion. I report to the COO. I have four senior TPMs under me and a coordination function across the engineering org.

I don't write code. I haven't shipped code in eleven years. I want to be clear about that up front because the kind of engineer who reads a profile like mine usually wants to know.

What I do — and what I've done at three aerospace companies before this — is I keep cross-functional aerospace programs from collapsing under their own coordination cost. I'm honest that "kept it from collapsing" is a hard thing to put on a resume because the failure mode is invisible. The way you know I did my job is the program shipped. The way you don't know is that without me it might have shipped anyway, six months later, with three avoidable rework cycles. There's no counterfactual that resolves this.

The specific things I'm pointing at, by program: the ATLAS-3 satellite I program-managed at my previous company, 2019-2022, was a constellation member that hit launch on schedule with a 3-week reserve. The previous bird in the same constellation was 11 months late. I think the program was on time partly because of me, and partly because of the lessons learned from the 11-month-late prior bird, and I am not going to claim I deserve all the credit for it. At Halcyon, I program-managed the ORION-2 mission in 2024 — 18 months from kickoff to launch, which is fast for our class — and I'm currently running ORION-4 which is mid-integration.

I'll tell you what I think I do specifically. I run interface-control-document review meetings differently than most TPMs do. I run them as adversarial design reviews rather than status checks. I make every IC engineer in the room defend their interface against a specific alternative I've researched in advance. The first three meetings each program are painful. After that the engineers come prepared because they know I've read the document and I have done my homework. The throughput of decisions improves. The number of "wait, you said what?" surprise meetings six months later goes down by — this is impressionistic — maybe half.

I cannot give you a metric for this. I have run six aerospace programs over twelve years and across all of them I do not have a clean A/B comparison against not-having-done-it. I have testimonials. The chief engineer of ATLAS-3 emailed me in 2023 to tell me that my IRD reviews had "saved the program." The COO at Halcyon hired me away from my previous job specifically on the basis of references from two named engineering directors. That's the evidence I have. It's not nothing. It's also not the kind of evidence engineers respect.

Honest about my own pattern: I don't ship artifacts. I shape decisions by being relentlessly prepared and by making the people in the room less polite to each other in ways that surface technical issues. I take political damage I think is worth taking. I'm probably more disliked at any given moment than the typical VP of programs, and I'm more trusted six months later than the typical VP of programs. I think those two things are causally connected.

I'm not sure if I'm actually any good at this or if I just survived three programs that other people got blamed for failing. I suspect both are true and I don't have the data to separate them.
