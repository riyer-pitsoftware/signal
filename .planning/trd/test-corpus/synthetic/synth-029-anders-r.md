# Anders R. — VP of Engineering, vertical SaaS (logistics)

## Background

VP of Engineering at Halewood Logistics Software, a vertical SaaS for mid-market freight brokers. ~190 people, ~50 in engineering. Two years in role. I have three directors under me — application, platform, and integrations — through them about 35 ICs.

I'm going to try to give you the version of my work that doesn't sound like every other VP-of-engineering profile, because I read those profiles too and they all sound the same.

What I genuinely do, in concrete time-blocks of a typical week:

About 30% of my time is in 1:1s, mostly with my directors and skip-level. The skip-levels are usually with senior staff engineers who are evaluating whether to leave. I don't always know that's why they took the meeting and they often don't either, but I've learned to read the conversational shape; about half the skip-levels I take this year are senior-engineers-in-soft-attrition. My job in those conversations is to figure out whether the friction is fixable, name what I think it is, and either commit to fixing it or be honest that I can't fix it on the timeline that matters to them.

About 20% of my time is on what I'd call commercial-engineering interface. We have three accounts that represent about 35% of revenue and they have engineering opinions because their internal IT teams are large. I am the engineering-side counterpart on those accounts when something is at stake bigger than what my customer-success-engineering team can handle. This year I had four of those conversations. Two were technical (architecture decisions about our integration shape with their TMS). Two were political (a pricing dispute that was being escalated to a "values" debate, which it wasn't really, and a contract-renewal where the customer wanted access to roadmap that we don't share publicly).

About 15% of my time is on hiring. We're not aggressively hiring, but we're consistently hiring. I sit in on the staff-and-above interview loops. I do the closing conversations on offers we're worried about losing. I've personally talked candidates out of accepting elsewhere four times in the past year, with mixed retention outcomes — three of the four are still here, one left after seven months for reasons that turned out to be exactly what she said they were when I was trying to convince her to take the offer.

About 15% of my time is in board / exec-team / cross-functional forums. Quarterly board prep, weekly exec-team meeting, monthly product-engineering syncs, and the variable load of whatever crisis is currently being managed at the exec level.

The remaining 20% is the operational work that has no good label: comp planning, performance-management decisions on edge cases my directors bring me, hiring-loop coordination for hard searches, and what I'd call "thinking time" that I have stopped feeling guilty about because the alternative is operational chaos.

I want to be honest that none of this maps cleanly to a deliverable. I have not personally shipped a piece of software in two years. I have not architected a system. I have not run an incident. The closest thing to a tangible artifact I produced in 2024 is the engineering-org operating model I rewrote in Q1 — a 12-page document that describes how decisions are made, escalated, and recorded across the four teams. My directors tell me they refer to it weekly. I cannot prove this is true. I have read it myself maybe three times since I wrote it.

I think the work I'm proudest of, in this role: I have hired three staff engineers I'm genuinely proud to have hired, one of whom probably saved us from a major outage in Q3 (he caught a database-migration issue in review that would have taken our largest customer's instance offline for ~6 hours; that's an estimate, the actual incident did not happen, so the counterfactual is real but unverifiable). I have transitioned one director out (correctly, in retrospect; the team morale data confirmed it). I have argued for and won a budget for a third platform engineer, which we are about to land.

What I cannot point to: anything I personally wrote, designed, or built that I would point to as my work-product. The work-products are decisions and people. The decisions are mostly invisible. The people are individually identifiable but their work isn't mine.

I am aware that this is the kind of self-description that any VP could write. I'd argue the difference is in the specifics — the "mostly senior engineers in soft attrition" frame for skip-levels, the 4-of-3-still-there hiring outcome, the operating-model document I cannot prove gets used. These are the kinds of granular evidence I have. They're not as legible as engineering work product. They are what the role produces.

If you're asking me what I'm best at: I am calibrated about what is and isn't fixable in an engineering org under specific budget and timeline constraints. I do not promise fixes I cannot deliver. I will say "this won't get better while I'm in this role and within these constraints" when that's true. My directors tell me this is unusual and they appreciate it. The CEO tells me it's politically inconvenient and he wishes I'd be more optimistic in board prep.
