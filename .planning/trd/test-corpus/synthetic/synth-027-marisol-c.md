# Marisol C. — Director of Web Engineering, healthtech

## Background

Director of Web Engineering at Cyrah Care, telehealth platform for chronic-disease management. ~480 people, ~95 in engineering. Three years in role. I have three managers under me — patient web, provider web, web platform — and through them about 24 engineers.

I want to give you the contradictory-praise version up front because it's clearer to start with.

The CEO and the chief product officer like working with me. The CEO has cited me in two company offsites as an example of "ship-it leadership." I get strong year-end reviews. I am on the comp band's upper third. The board has been told twice in QBRs that I'm doing the role well.

The user-experience research team and the design organization do not like working with me. Two of three senior designers we've hired since I joined have left within 18 months, and at least one of them named me directly in their exit interview as a contributing factor. The director of design has filed two complaints to my VP about my engineering organization "running over" her function on cross-discipline launches. There is a real pattern.

The honest version of why this happens: I prioritize launch-readiness over design-readiness. When a design has a piece I think is going to slow the engineering team — and I'm typically right about which pieces those are — I push back, hard, in cross-functional reviews. I argue for a v1-without-the-piece. I propose simpler interactions when the design has a complex one. I am, by my count, the person who most often says "let's ship without that and add it later" in our cross-functional product-engineering-design forum.

I think this is mostly the right behavior for the company. Our launches have been on time three quarters running. Our quarterly user-engagement metrics are improving. The simpler interaction is, in my experience, almost always good enough for the user, and the cost of the complex interaction is a delay the company can ill afford.

But it is also not the same as good design partnership. The designers I've worked with feel — correctly — that their craft is not being respected. Their good design choices are being downgraded for engineering convenience. The pieces I cut to ship-on-time are pieces they cared about. And — this is the part I'm working on — sometimes the simpler version *is* worse, and I don't always know it. The designers are the ones who know it. I'm the one who decided it.

The exec leadership doesn't see this because they see launches landing. The design org sees it intensely because they live the cost.

What I think I'm best at: making the call between "this design element is critical" and "this design element is nice-to-have," under deadline pressure, when the org wants to ship and the design team wants to refine. What I'm worst at: making that call in a way that doesn't damage the design team's trust in me.

I have tried, this past year, to fix this. I now require that any cut I propose be accompanied by my own articulation of *why the design org cares about it*, in their language. The director of design tells me this has helped a little. She also tells me it hasn't fixed the underlying problem, which is that I am still the person making the cut and they are still the people losing the argument.

What I am not sure about: whether the role I'm in fundamentally requires this damage and the right move is to accept it as a structural cost, or whether there's a version of the role where I make the same calls without the damage and I just haven't figured out how to do it yet. I do not have evidence either way and I am genuinely uncertain.

I should add — the engineering team likes me. Retention on my team is good. My managers tell me they value how clearly I prioritize. The friction is across the discipline-boundary, not within it. That fits the contradictory-praise pattern: from inside engineering I look like a clear leader, from outside engineering I look like a barrier.
