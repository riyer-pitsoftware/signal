# Amara W. — VP of Research Engineering, govtech

## Background

VP of Research Engineering at Civic Signal Labs, a 501(c)(3) doing computational social-science work for state and county governments. Around 90 people, ~25 in research engineering. I report to the executive director. I joined three years ago and the role has evolved since I joined.

I want to start with how I describe myself when someone asks at a conference, because I think that version is partly real and partly a story I tell, and I'd rather you see both.

The version I tell: I'm a research engineer who builds the bridge between academic computational social-science and operational deployment. I came up in academia — PhD in poli sci with a heavy methods focus, two years as a research scientist at a university affiliated polling lab — and I've spent the last eight years in industry-adjacent roles where the work is to make academically-rigorous methods actually usable by non-academic stakeholders. I describe my craft as "production-grade methods for non-production-grade users."

That version is mostly true and it does describe what I do most days.

The version that is more honest: I'm a former academic who was not on tenure track and made a virtue of it. I left poli sci because I wasn't going to get a tenure-track offer at a school I wanted to take. I told myself that story for the first three years out of academia. Then I told myself it was about impact. Then I told myself it was about craft. None of these are wrong. None of these are the original reason. The original reason was that I needed an income that wasn't six adjunct courses spread across two cities.

I think the work I do is meaningfully better than the work I would have done in academia, but I cannot prove that and I am suspicious of my own argument for it.

What I actually do: I lead a team of seven research engineers who turn academic methodology — Bayesian hierarchical modeling, MRP for small-area estimation, network-causal inference — into pipelines that a state-government data analyst can run, monitor, and explain to a legislator. We deploy in production for three states and twelve counties currently. Our biggest deployment last year was a redistricting-evaluation toolkit for a state legislature; we built a system that scores redistricting plans on contiguity, compactness, and partisan-symmetry metrics, with full propagation of uncertainty from voter-file noise. That was 14 months of work, four researchers and three engineers, and it ran during the actual redistricting cycle. The legislature didn't end up using its outputs in the way we'd hoped; they used the contiguity check and ignored the partisan-symmetry analysis because it produced a politically inconvenient finding. That's a real outcome. We didn't change anyone's mind. We made the inconvenient finding harder to deny.

I think my career has a pattern that I'm willing to claim: I am unusually good at translating between academic-methodological precision and stakeholder-readable outputs. Most academics who try this do too much methodology and lose the audience. Most product engineers do too little methodology and lose the rigor. I sit in the middle, but it's a narrow band.

I am also unusually bad at knowing when to push organizationally for the work to matter. I have shipped four projects in three years where the deployed system was technically sound and the legislative outcome was — null. I think that's mostly the political environment, not me. I also think saying that is partly an alibi.

If you ask me what I'm best at, the answer I give in interviews is "translating rigor into action." The answer I give myself is "I'm good at the technical-political middle layer that academia and industry both ignore, and I've stopped expecting either of them to recognize the work I do, which has made me a better craftsperson and possibly a worse career-strategist."
