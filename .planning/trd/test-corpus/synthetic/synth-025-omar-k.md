# Omar K. — VP of Engineering, consumer mobile (commerce)

## Background

VP of Engineering at Reefcrest, a consumer commerce app — small-business marketplace, around 350 people, ~85 in engineering. I have four directors under me — mobile, web, services, infra — and through them around 65 ICs. I report to the CTO. Three years in role.

I want to start with what I think the load-bearing question is for someone like me: what does a VP of Engineering actually do, and how would you tell whether they're doing it well? I do not have a clean answer. Most of my evidence is indirect. I'm going to give you what I have.

What I do not do: I do not write code. I have not written code outside a private hobby project in five years. I do not architect systems. I do not run incident response. My directors do those things and I would mostly get in their way if I tried.

What I do is — and I'm going to be specific even if it sounds soft — I shape decisions, I shape relationships, and I take the political damage that protects my directors from having to take it. Specifically:

(1) I argued for and got the org structure we have now. When I joined, mobile, web, and services were all reporting through a single engineering manager who reported to the CTO. The CTO didn't have the bandwidth and the manager was burning out. I argued for the four-pillar structure with directors above each pillar, hired three of the four directors over my first year, promoted one from within. I cannot prove this structure is better than the alternative because I don't have a counterfactual. I can tell you our quarterly delivery against product roadmap went from a 65% on-time rate to 88% in the year after the restructure. I can also tell you that the restructure happened to coincide with a product-management restructure that was probably as important. I cannot disentangle them.

(2) I have been the person who carries a hard product-vs-engineering disagreement to the CEO. We had three of these in the last 18 months. One was a feature that product wanted shipped against a timeline that would have required us to skip our security-review process. I escalated, got the timeline pushed by 11 weeks, the feature shipped. The other two — a pricing-experiment decision and a scope-cut on an ML-personalization feature — I escalated and lost. The product VP got the decision she wanted in both cases. In one of those losses I think I was right; in the other I think I was wrong.

(3) I run our weekly engineering-leadership meeting in a way that I think materially differs from how it ran before. I do not run it as a status meeting. I run it as a forum where my directors are required to identify the decision they're stuck on this week and either resolve it in the room or get the help they need. I cannot prove this is better than other forums; I can tell you that my directors tell me, in 1:1s, that this is the meeting they get the most out of. Two of them have told me they keep a list throughout the week of what to bring to it.

(4) I take a lot of the upward-political-management hit. The CFO does not like our engineering-spend trajectory. I have had — I think — eight scheduled-and-difficult conversations with the CFO this year about hiring, vendor spend, and infra costs. My directors have not had those conversations. That is intentional. The reason they have not had those conversations is I did not want them spending their political capital on conversations the CFO would have anyway, regardless of who he was talking to.

What I cannot point to: I cannot point to a specific deliverable I shipped. I cannot point to a specific architectural decision I made. I cannot show you a Github contributions graph that says I am operating at VP-altitude. The artifacts of my work are decisions other people then carried out, relationships other people then operated within, and political weather that other people did not have to weather.

I am aware that this is exactly how a low-output VP would also describe themselves. The honest answer to "how do you tell which kind of VP you are" is: you ask the directors. My directors tell me they want me to stay in the role. The CFO tells the CTO he wishes I were better at cost control. The CTO has given me a strong-positive review three years running. None of these are conclusive but the aggregate is what I have.

What I think I'm best at: making my directors better at being directors. What I'm not sure about: whether the company is getting out of me what they're paying for, because the evidence I have for what they're getting is weaker than the evidence engineers get for their own work. This is, I think, a real epistemic problem of the role.
