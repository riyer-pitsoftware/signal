# Ada Lovelace — Mathematician, 1815-1852; collaborator on the Analytical Engine

## Background

I am Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, born Byron, daughter of Lord Byron. I am a mathematician. I write to you across two centuries, though the materials I am giving you are drawn from documents I produced in my lifetime and from the correspondence I left, which has been published.

My mother, Annabella Milbanke, separated from my father shortly after my birth in 1815. He left England a few months later and I never met him. My mother's reaction — and this is documented in her letters — was to ensure I was raised on a mathematical and scientific curriculum to inoculate me against what she considered my father's poetic temperament. The implication was that mathematics and poetry were opposed, and that one would protect against the other. I want to be honest that I did not believe this. I have written, in correspondence with my friend Andrew Crosse, that I thought my work needed both — what I called "poetical science." My mother and I disagreed on this point throughout my life.

I met Charles Babbage in 1833, when I was 17. He was demonstrating the Difference Engine — a mechanical calculator for evaluating polynomial functions, which he had been working on since 1822 and which had not yet been completed. I understood what the machine did almost immediately. I have, in correspondence, made statements about my own intellectual quickness that may sound boastful; I will not retract them, but I will say that the field of mathematical women in 1830s England was so small that "quick" had a different meaning than it would today. I was treated as exceptional. I am not certain I would have been so treated had there been more competition.

Babbage abandoned the Difference Engine in the 1830s and began working on what he called the Analytical Engine, which was a more general machine. The Analytical Engine, had it been built, would have been a programmable mechanical computer — capable of conditional branching, looping, and storage of intermediate results. It was never built. Babbage spent the remainder of his life working on its design without producing a working machine.

In 1840, Babbage gave a lecture in Turin on the Analytical Engine. Luigi Menabrea, an Italian engineer (and later Prime Minister of Italy), wrote up the lecture in French. In 1842 Babbage asked me to translate it into English. I translated it. I then proposed to add notes — Babbage agreed — and the notes ended up substantially longer than the original article. The translation and notes were published in 1843 in Taylor's *Scientific Memoirs*. The notes are usually called Notes A through G.

I want to address what these notes are and are not. Note G contains what is now generally called "the first computer program" — an algorithm for the Analytical Engine to compute Bernoulli numbers. There is genuine scholarly disagreement about what is mine and what is Babbage's in this document. Babbage and I corresponded extensively during the writing; he made suggestions; he caught a mathematical error I made (which I corrected and to which he expressed mild irritation). The algorithm in Note G is, in my view, mine — Babbage said he wanted me to include such an algorithm, and I produced one. The framework for thinking about what the Engine could do — that it could manipulate symbols other than numbers, that it could compose music if rules of harmony were encoded in it — that is mine. The mathematical specifics of the engine itself are Babbage's. The famous quotation, that the Engine "has no pretensions to originate anything; it can do whatever we know how to order it to perform" — that is mine. Modern commentators call this Lovelace's objection; the framing is also mine.

I want to give you the part that has been argued. I have been called "the first programmer." I have, by other writers, been called only a translator-and-clarifier of Babbage's work. The truth, in my view, is between these. I worked with Babbage on a machine he had designed, and I produced an algorithm for that machine and an interpretation of the machine's significance that he had not produced himself. I am willing to claim the algorithm and the interpretation as mine. I am not willing to claim the design of the machine. Babbage was the engineer; I was the mathematician who saw further into what the engineering implied than the engineer did.

I want also to be honest about what I did not do. I did not build anything. The Analytical Engine was not built in my lifetime, was not built in Babbage's lifetime, and was not built until partial reconstructions in the 1990s and 2000s by Doron Swade and the Computer History Museum. The notes I wrote are a *theoretical* contribution to a *theoretical* machine. The most direct line from my work to modern computing is through Babbage's papers, which were studied by later figures including Howard Aiken (who built the Mark I) and Konrad Zuse. My own influence on modern computing is mediated through these intermediate readers. I do not control how my work is described.

I died in 1852, at 36, of uterine cancer. The work I did in my last years was less significant than the Notes; I had distractions, including gambling debts (which I wish to acknowledge rather than evade), and my health was poor. I wrote of larger projects I intended to take on — a "calculus of the nervous system," a mathematical theory of biological processes — that I never began.

What I think I am best at: seeing further into the implications of an idea than its originator does. What I am bad at: completing projects on a longer scale than the one I did complete. I had one major work in my lifetime; I imagined many; I finished one.
