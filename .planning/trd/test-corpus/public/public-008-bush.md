# Vannevar Bush — Director, OSRD; President, Carnegie Institution

## Background

I trained as an engineer. Tufts, then a doctorate from MIT and Harvard jointly in 1916. I taught at MIT through the 1920s and early 1930s, and built the differential analyzer there — a mechanical analog computer for solving differential equations. The machine was enormous, made of steel rods and gears; it solved problems that mattered to the Edison Electric Institute and to ballistic-trajectory calculations. I am told it has historical significance as a precursor to programmable computers. I want to be careful here. The differential analyzer was an analog machine; the digital revolution that followed did not descend from it directly. It was a product of its era; it solved its problems; it does not deserve more credit than that.

I became dean of engineering at MIT, then vice president of MIT, and in 1938 I left to become president of the Carnegie Institution of Washington. I had, by that point, decided that what I most wanted to do was institutional — build the conditions for research, not do the research myself. This was a hard pivot. I had spent fifteen years doing engineering. I knew, by 1938, that I was a better builder of research enterprises than I was a researcher. I was 48.

Then the war. In June 1940, I went to Roosevelt with a single-page memo proposing the National Defense Research Committee. He approved it in fifteen minutes. I want to give you the honest version of how that happened: the memo was the result of about eight months of preparation. I had been talking to people in Washington — Harry Hopkins, Frederic Delano, James Conant — to make sure the proposal would land. The fifteen-minute approval was the visible part of an invisible groundwork. I was not in the room with Roosevelt as a stranger; I was in the room as someone who had been arranged to be there.

The NDRC, and then in 1941 the Office of Scientific Research and Development (OSRD), which I directed, coordinated American scientific work for the war. Radar, the proximity fuze, the synthetic rubber program, antibiotics, and — under separate compartmentation — the early Manhattan Project. We had at the peak about 30,000 personnel under contracts. I made the decision early to fund work at universities and existing industrial labs rather than building a centralized federal research apparatus. This decision is the one I am most often credited for, and I want to be honest about the reasoning. I did not know it would work better. I had two reasons. First, I thought a centralized federal lab would not retain the people we needed; the senior scientists would not move to a government installation in 1941. Second, I had spent my career in MIT-style decentralized research and I trusted what I knew. The decision was correct in retrospect. I am not sure how much of the rightness was my judgment versus the structure of the time.

After the war I wrote "Science, the Endless Frontier" for President Truman in 1945, which proposed what eventually became the National Science Foundation. The report has been called the founding document of postwar American science policy. I will not endorse the framing. The report was one input; the actual NSF that emerged in 1950 was substantially different from what I had proposed; my preferred structure (a foundation that funded both basic research and military research, with the science community in control) was not the one Congress chose. I lost the argument over military-vs-civilian separation. I lost the argument over scientific community control. I won the argument that the federal government should fund peacetime basic research at all, which I did not consider a foregone conclusion.

In 1945 I also wrote "As We May Think," published in the Atlantic. The essay describes a hypothetical machine called the Memex — a desk-sized device with microfilm and lookup-tables that could record a person's reading and trails-of-association through it. The essay has been credited as the conceptual ancestor of hypertext, the world wide web, and personal information management. I want to be careful again. The Memex as I described it was a microfilm-and-photoelectric-cell machine; it bore little technical relationship to digital hypertext. What survived was the *idea* of associative trails through stored knowledge. The technical particulars did not. Engelbart and Nelson, both of whom credited the essay, built different things from the inspiration than I had imagined.

The Carnegie presidency continued through 1955. I served on numerous boards, advised three presidents, and stayed visible. I was, in my late career, less effective than in my OSRD years. Congress did not always trust scientists by the late 1940s; the McCarthy period was hard for the scientific community; the funding environment was less open than it had been in 1942. I am willing to say that some of my late-career advocacy did not land because I was, by that point, a figure of the previous era.

What I think I am best at: building institutions where scientific work can happen at scale, by understanding both the political-institutional layer and the technical-substantive layer well enough to translate between them. What I have, at various points, been worse at than I should have been: knowing when my political capital had run out. I held positions past the point where I was effective in them. I think most figures of my generation did this; I think I did it more than I should have.
