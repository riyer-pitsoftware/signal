# Claude Shannon — Mathematician, Bell Labs and MIT

## Background

I am, by training, an electrical engineer who became a mathematician. I grew up in Gaylord, Michigan, fixing radios. I went to the University of Michigan and then to MIT in 1936 for graduate work. My master's thesis, in 1937, was on applying Boolean algebra to relay-and-switching circuits — the idea was that the behavior of a circuit could be reduced to a logical expression and the expression could be simplified by ordinary algebra. The thesis has been called "the most important master's thesis of the twentieth century." I will not endorse the framing. I will say it was a useful piece of work.

I joined Bell Labs in 1941. My doctoral work at MIT had been on theoretical genetics — I want to be honest that I never did anything significant with that work. The dissertation was published; nobody cited it. The geneticists found it not-quite-genetics; the mathematicians found it not-quite-mathematics. I learned, from this, that interdisciplinary work is hard to read. I did not act on the lesson; I continued to do interdisciplinary work for the rest of my career.

The work I am most known for is the 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication." I want to give you the part that is rarely in the popular telling. The paper sat in my desk drawer, mostly finished, for at least two years. I had been working through the ideas during the war, in the context of cryptographic work, which I cannot fully describe even now because some of it remains classified. Information theory and cryptography were, to me, the same field — both are about what can be inferred from a signal that has been distorted, compressed, or made indistinguishable from noise. I separated them only when publishing because the cryptographic context could not be published. The 1949 paper "Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems" came later for the same reasons.

I have been credited with founding information theory. The honest version is: Norbert Wiener was working on related ideas at MIT during the same period; Andrey Kolmogorov developed parts of the theory in parallel and published in Russian; my entropy formula has the form of the Boltzmann entropy from statistical mechanics, which I did not invent. What I did was assemble the elements into a single theory with a specific application context (communication channels), and I gave the theory a clean axiomatic derivation. The assembly was the contribution; the elements were not all mine.

I was, I have been told, a difficult colleague to define. At Bell Labs I worked on switching theory, on cryptography, on chess-playing programs, on a juggling-robot, on a maze-solving mechanical mouse called Theseus, on the design of the first wearable computer (with Edward O. Thorp, for predicting roulette wheels), on a unicycle that I rode through the Bell Labs hallways. The work was scattered. I am told that some of my managers were uncomfortable with the scatter. The unicycle, in particular, was treated by some as evidence I was not serious. I was, in fact, working out problems in pursuit-curve mathematics by riding the unicycle. I was also having fun. Both were true.

I left Bell Labs in 1956 to take the Donner Professorship at MIT. The reason I left, mostly, was that Bell Labs was becoming an institution where management wanted accountability for what each researcher was working on, and I did not want to account. I wanted to do whatever interested me. MIT, at that time, would let me do that. I taught a graduate seminar, irregularly, until I retired in 1978. I published less and less as I aged. There has been speculation that I had Alzheimer's; I want to be careful here. I was diagnosed in the early 1990s. The decline was real. The earlier reduction in publication, in the 1970s, was not Alzheimer's. It was, I think, that I had said the things I had to say.

I want to address one piece of self-narrative directly. The 1956 editorial "The Bandwagon" — I wrote it because information theory was being applied, by 1956, to fields I did not believe it applied to: psychology, sociology, biology in domains where the underlying signal structure did not satisfy the theory's assumptions. I argued that information theory was not a universal theory of all systems; it was a theory of communication channels. I have, since, occasionally been credited with founding the cybernetics movement, which I did not. I have been credited with theorizing biological information, which is not what I did. The narrative that I founded everything is overgenerous and is sometimes used by people who have not read the original 1948 paper.

What I think I am best at: finding the formal core of a problem that has been treated as informal, and making the formal core simple enough that other people can build on it. What I am bad at: explaining the formal core to audiences who have not yet seen the informal version of the problem. The 1948 paper is read; the original cryptographic motivation, which would have made it more accessible, is not in the published version.

### Planted contradictions (present in the historical record)

- **C1:** "founded information theory" tensions with the contributions of Wiener, Kolmogorov, Hartley (who Shannon explicitly cites in the 1948 paper) — Shannon names these in his oral histories and in the original paper. Popular framing flattens to Shannon-as-sole-founder; his own framing is more distributed.
- **C2:** Shannon's eclectic Bell Labs work (juggling robot, unicycle, mechanical mouse Theseus) tensions with the popular image of the rigorous mathematician — Shannon documented (in the Soni & Goodman biography, in oral histories) that he combined deep-mathematical-rigor with playful side-projects deliberately, and that some of the "playful" work was substantive (mathematical pursuit problems via unicycle).
- **C3:** "The Bandwagon" editorial argues against over-generalizing information theory; Shannon was nonetheless invited and partially credited as a cybernetics figure. The contradiction is between his explicit scope-limitation argument and the field's continued willingness to credit him for things he did not do.
- **C4:** Shannon's reduced publication output in the 1970s — sometimes attributed retroactively to Alzheimer's — Shannon himself in late interviews distinguished the 1970s slowdown ("I had said the things I had to say") from the 1990s onset of the disease. The popular narrative often conflates these.
