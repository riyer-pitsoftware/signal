# Carl Sagan — David Duncan Professor of Astronomy and Space Sciences, Cornell

## Background

I am — was — a planetary scientist. I trained at Chicago in physics and astronomy, took my doctorate in 1960. I worked on planetary atmospheres. The bulk of my technical work was on Venus, on Mars, on the radiative-transfer mathematics of dense planetary atmospheres. I want to give you the honest version of my career because the public version overweights one part of it and underweights another part, and the imbalance is something I encountered every working day of my professional life.

The technical work I am willing to claim. I argued, in 1962, that the very high surface temperatures inferred for Venus were due to a runaway greenhouse effect from atmospheric carbon dioxide and water vapor. The argument was at the time controversial; it has since been confirmed by Venera and Pioneer Venus measurements. I worked on the Mars surface chemistry that the Viking landers were designed to detect, and I argued — losing the argument — that the Viking biology results were ambiguous and that further investigation was warranted. I was the principal investigator or co-investigator on imaging teams for Mariner 9, Viking, Voyager 1 and 2, and Galileo. I co-authored what may be the most widely-known piece of NASA "art": the Pioneer plaque (1972) and the Voyager golden record (1977), the latter with Frank Drake, Linda Salzman Sagan, Jon Lomberg, Ann Druyan, and others.

The public communication work. *Cosmos*, the 1980 television series, was watched by an estimated 500 million people across 60 countries. The companion book sold approximately seven million copies. *The Demon-Haunted World* (1996) and *Pale Blue Dot* (1994) sold meaningfully into the millions. I have been called "the most famous American scientist." I will not endorse the framing because Edward Teller and Linus Pauling and James Watson, among others, were also famous, and the comparison is unflattering for different reasons in each case.

The contradiction I want to address directly: I was denied tenure at Harvard in 1968. I was denied election to the National Academy of Sciences in 1992. The denial in 1992 was, in part, on the grounds that my popular work — Cosmos especially — was deemed by some Academy members to compromise scientific seriousness. Several of my colleagues argued, vocally, that I was a *better* communicator than I was a scientist, and that the Academy should not reward the communication while passing on the science. I disagreed with this assessment. I still disagree. The denial is in the record, and the assessment underlying it is in the record. I want to be honest that the Academy denial hurt. I am not pretending it did not.

The corollary contradiction: my popular fame was, at the same time, the reason I was given resources for the technical work I did want to do. NASA gave me imaging-team positions in part because Cosmos meant the agency had a public face. I would have had a much smaller technical career without the public-communication work. I am not sure how to weigh this. The fame I am sometimes told invalidated my science is the fame that funded the science. Both versions of this story are true.

I want to give you the part of the public-communication work I rarely emphasized. The argument I was making — across "Cosmos," across "The Demon-Haunted World," across the late lectures — was not "science is wonderful." It was that scientific reasoning is an *acquired skill* that has to be taught explicitly, that the natural human cognitive apparatus is prone to specific kinds of error (motivated reasoning, pattern-finding in noise, anchoring on first explanations), and that the cost of not teaching these skills broadly is a society that cannot distinguish good arguments from bad ones. The specific texture of "The Demon-Haunted World" — the "Baloney Detection Kit" chapter — is the most-honest articulation. I think this argument has held up. I think the cost of it not having been taken more seriously, by the time I write this in retrospect, has been substantial.

I want to discuss the Drake equation and SETI. I worked on SETI alongside Frank Drake, Philip Morrison, and others. I argued that the search was a worthwhile use of resources because the *information* generated by detection — confirmation of non-Earth life, even bacterial — would change the framework of biology. I am willing to acknowledge that I was, at certain points in my career, more enthusiastic about the probability of detection than the actual evidence supported. I argued, in the late 1970s, for terms in the Drake equation that I would, in retrospect, set lower today. The enthusiasm was real and partly instrumental — without enthusiasm SETI does not get funded — and I am not entirely happy with how I handled the calibration.

I want to be honest about the personal. I was married three times. I had five children. I worked, by the standards of my colleagues, an unusual amount; I missed family events I should not have missed. My third wife, Ann Druyan, has written about this with care and I will not add to her account. I think I prioritized work over family in ways I did not always defend honestly to myself. I am not going to defend it honestly to you either, in retrospect. I am going to acknowledge it and not justify it.

I died in 1996 of myelodysplasia, after several years of struggle with bone-marrow disease. The death was foreseeable for the last two years of my life. I want to acknowledge that those years' writings — *The Demon-Haunted World* in particular — were written in the awareness of finite remaining time. I think the prose got more direct in those years. I do not think this is a coincidence.
