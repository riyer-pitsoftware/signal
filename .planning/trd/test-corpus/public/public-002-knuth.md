# Donald Knuth — Professor Emeritus, Stanford; author of TAOCP

## Background

I'm a computer scientist. I am known for two large works: The Art of Computer Programming, which I started writing in 1962 and which I am still writing in volumes, and the TeX typesetting system, which I built between 1977 and 1989 because I could not stand what was happening to the typography of my own books.

I went to Case Institute of Technology as an undergraduate in physics. I switched to mathematics. I had paid my way through college partly by writing — I wrote a compiler for a minor computer manufacturer called Burroughs, the ALGOL compiler, and they paid me $5,500 for it, which was meaningful money in 1960. I have been told, since, that this was a fair price for one of the better ALGOL compilers of that period. I'd like to be careful about claiming I knew what I was doing. I had read Backus's papers. I tried things. Most of the things worked.

I started Volume 1 of The Art of Computer Programming in 1962 because Addison-Wesley asked me to write a book about compilers. I told them I would, and I wrote 3,000 pages, and we agreed it was not a book, it was a series. I planned seven volumes. I am, in 2025, working on Volume 4 in fascicles. I have not finished. I have been writing this book for sixty-three years and I have not finished. I do not expect to finish in my lifetime.

I want to be honest about the TAOCP timeline because the simple version makes me sound either heroic or absurd. The honest version is: I underestimated the field. When I started in 1962, I thought I knew most of what should be in the book. By 1968 when Volume 1 came out I knew I was wrong. The field grew faster than I could cover it. I made a decision in the early 1990s to slow down further to do the work properly. I have not regretted that decision but I have, occasionally, regretted being the kind of person who can make it.

The TeX project. In 1977, my publisher sent me galleys of Volume 2 of TAOCP, which they had typeset using a new photographic process. The mathematics looked terrible. I remember being unable to read it. I decided I would write my own typesetting program. I had a sabbatical and I thought I'd spend a year on it. I spent the next twelve years on it, on and off — the program itself, the METAFONT companion, the Computer Modern fonts, everything. I shipped TeX 3.0 in 1989 and I declared it frozen. Since then it accepts only bug-fix releases, with version numbers that approach pi. We are, in 2025, at TeX 3.141592653 or thereabouts.

I want to give you the part that is harder to talk about. I stopped using email in 1990. I made this decision because I had become important enough that I could not, in any week, answer all the email I received. I had to choose between writing The Art of Computer Programming and answering email. I chose. The cost has been real — I have lost touch with people I should not have lost touch with, and I have probably missed important things. The benefit has been that I have written the books. I do not know whether the trade was correct. I do know that I made the trade, and I would make it again.

I have been told, my entire career, that I am too detail-oriented. The MMIX architecture I designed for the third edition of TAOCP took several years on its own. The TeX pursuit-of-the-perfect-line-break algorithm — I rewrote it several times. The numerical accuracy guarantees in the original Computer Modern fonts. I do not believe these were excessive at the time I worked on them. I believe, looking back, that some of them were excessive. I am not sure which is which.

I think people who have read my work mostly think I am a careful writer of mathematics. I think people who have worked with me directly know that I am also someone who can be hard to negotiate with on details that matter to me. My students, and especially my collaborators on TeX, can tell stories. The stories are mostly true. I am not going to deny them.

What I think I have done that has lasted: the analysis-of-algorithms framework that TAOCP made standard, the literate-programming method, TeX itself, and a generation of students whose work I believe has surpassed mine in their respective specialties. The first three I can point to. The fourth I cannot prove and I would not try.

### Planted contradictions (present in the historical record)

- **C1:** Knuth's stated identity as a careful writer of mathematics tensions with his self-described difficulty in collaboration. He acknowledges this in "Coders at Work" and the Stanford oral history. The contradiction is that the same care that makes the work durable makes the collaboration friction-laden.
- **C2:** Knuth's 1990 cessation of email "to write the books" tensions with his ongoing publication of essays, fascicles, and Christmas Tree lectures (Stanford archives). He is not a recluse; he has continued public-facing work. The simplification "Knuth doesn't use email" reads as withdrawal; the actual pattern is more selective public engagement.
- **C3:** "I have been writing this book for sixty-three years and I have not finished" tensions with the cultural framing of TAOCP as a finished landmark — Knuth himself names the unfinishedness as the actual state, while readers often refer to it as a complete reference. Self-correcting against the popular narrative.
