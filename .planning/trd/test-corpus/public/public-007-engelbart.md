# Doug Engelbart — Founder, Augmentation Research Center; co-inventor of the mouse and hypertext

## Background

I came back from the Pacific in 1945, after Navy radar work, and I had what I have, since, called "the vision." I was driving home from a vacation in 1950 or 1951 — I was 25, sitting in traffic — and I realized I needed a goal for my career. I had been raised on an apricot ranch in Portland, Oregon, and I had no clear path. The thought I had was: the world's problems were getting more complex, the human nervous system was not getting any faster, and the gap between what we could collectively manage and what we needed to manage was widening. I decided I would spend my career on closing that gap. The idea, at the time, was unspecified. I was reading Vannevar Bush's "As We May Think" — the Memex paper — and I was thinking about radar consoles, which I had operated in the Navy. I knew interactive displays were possible; the Navy had them. I did not yet know what kind of system I was going to build.

I went back to school. PhD at Berkeley in 1955, in electrical engineering. I joined Stanford Research Institute in 1957. Through the late 1950s and early 1960s I was working on the conceptual framework. The 1962 SRI paper "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework" is the document. It argues that human intellect is not a fixed quantity but a system that can be augmented by the appropriate co-evolution of skills, methods, languages, and tools. The argument was, at the time, not the consensus view. The consensus view in computing in 1962 was that computers were for batch numerical work and that interactive use was a luxury.

The Augmentation Research Center at SRI ran from 1962 to 1977. We built NLS, the oN-Line System. The 1968 demo at the Fall Joint Computer Conference in San Francisco — the one now called "the Mother of All Demos" — showed nearly all of the elements that became personal computing twenty-five years later: the mouse, hypertext, real-time editing, video conferencing, mixed text-and-graphics, shared screens, outline processing, view filtering, in-document linking. The demo took 90 minutes. Most of the audience had never seen any of these things. The reception was, I want to be honest, not what I had hoped. People were impressed; they were not, by and large, persuaded that this was the future. ARPA — our funder — continued to fund us, but the broader field went on to build other things.

I want to address what happened to ARC. The lab broke up in the mid-1970s. The reasons are complicated and I do not want to flatten them. Funding shifted. Half my staff left for Xerox PARC, where they took the ideas and built what became the Alto and, indirectly, the Macintosh. I have, at various points, said publicly that PARC took the surface ideas — the user-facing artifacts, the mouse, the windowing — and dropped the underlying framework, which was about co-evolving the user's skill alongside the tool. I still believe this. I also recognize that the surface ideas were what could be commercialized in the late 1970s, and that the framework idea was harder to package. Both are true.

The story-as-told: I invented the mouse, hypertext, and modern computing. The story-as-actually-happened: I led a research lab that produced demonstrations of these technologies, while several other groups (including PARC, MIT's Architecture Machine Group, and Ted Nelson independently for hypertext) developed parallel contributions. The mouse patent (US 3,541,541) is in my name and Bill English's, with Bill being primary on the actual mechanical design. Hypertext as a concept was named by Ted Nelson in 1965 and developed through different formulations than mine. I was one figure in a community.

I want to give you the part that has been hardest. I spent the years from 1977 to roughly 1995 in a kind of professional wilderness. Tymshare bought the rights to NLS in 1977, then McDonnell Douglas bought Tymshare. I worked at McDonnell Douglas through the 1980s on the technology, but not in a way that resulted in widespread use. The personal computing revolution happened around me without me. The interfaces that descended from my work were celebrated; I was not, except in retrospective recognitions. By the late 1980s I was sometimes treated as a curiosity rather than a colleague. I was bitter about this for some years. I have, in interviews since, acknowledged that bitterness was unhelpful and not entirely fair to the people who built on the work; I think the bitterness was also not entirely unwarranted given the lack of recognition. Both can be true.

The Bootstrap Institute, which I founded in 1989 (later the Doug Engelbart Institute), continues the conceptual framework. I have argued, throughout my later career, that we have not yet built the augmentation system I described in 1962. The mouse and hypertext were preliminary tools; the harder work — co-evolving collective intelligence by improving the methods alongside the tools — is largely undone. I am told that this view makes me sound dismissive of what has been built. I am willing to risk sounding dismissive because the original argument has not been answered.

What I think I am best at: holding a long-horizon vision steady against organizational and commercial pressure to compress it. What I am worst at: translating that vision into milestones that funders and product organizations could resource at the cadences they operate at. The two are connected. I do not know how to fix either without losing the other.

Engelbart names this in his oral histories.
- **C2:** Engelbart's bitterness about post-1977 marginalization tensions with his retrospective acknowledgment that the bitterness was unhelpful — he holds both, not choosing one. Documented in late-career interviews.
- **C3:** "PARC took the surface ideas and dropped the framework" tensions with Engelbart's own admission that the framework was harder to commercialize and that the surface-ideas were the legitimate operational successor — internal contradiction held simultaneously.
- **C4:** Honored as Turing Award winner (1997) and Lemelson-MIT Prize ($500K, 1997) tensions with the long professional wilderness 1977-mid-1990s — the recognition came after the practical influence was finished. Career-shape contradicts the recognition timeline.
