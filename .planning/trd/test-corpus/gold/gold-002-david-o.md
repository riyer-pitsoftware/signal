# David O. — Engineering director who is actually a staff IC

## Background

Officially I'm a director of engineering at a mid-market logistics company, around 600 people total, around 80 in engineering. I report to the VP. Four managers report to me, and through them about 35 engineers across four teams: routing, dispatch, shipper-portal, and integrations.

My calendar would tell you I'm a manager. Mostly 1:1s, prio meetings, hiring loops, the usual. My git history would tell you something different. Last quarter I authored 41 PRs. Not all of them shipped — some were exploratory, some were "show the team what I think this should look like." But I can name every architecture decision in routing made in the last 18 months because most of them came from me, even when a team member's name is on the design doc.

I came up technical. Spent 9 years as IC, made staff at 31, was an architect at two B2B SaaS companies before this. The director title was an offer this CEO made to get me to leave the previous job — "you can build the platform you want, you'll have headcount." I took it.

Here's the pattern that actually defines my work, across the last three roles: I take a stalled platform project and I ship it in six months by cutting scope aggressively. At the SaaS in 2019 it was a multi-tenant rewrite that two staff engineers had been at for 18 months. I took it, killed three "must-have" features, shipped 80% in five months, and the remaining 20% never mattered. At my previous company in 2021 it was a billing migration. Same pattern: kill features, ship the spine, walk away. Here it was the routing rewrite — same playbook, same six-month timeline.

I'm not really managing my managers. I'm using them as a force multiplier to keep teams shipping while I cut scope. Two of my reports have left in the last 18 months. Both told me, in their exit interviews, that they felt I was too involved in technical details for someone with my title. The HR business partner agreed with them. The VP didn't.

If you're asking me what I do well, I'd say I build high-performing teams that ship. If you're asking me what I actually do, I'd say I run interference between product expectations and engineering reality, and I cut hard when I have to.
